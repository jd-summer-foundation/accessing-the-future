# Methods

We model the likelihood that in its first twenty years a new dwelling is occupied by a household that includes a person with disability and, as our primary focus, a person with a physical disability. In addition, we simulate the proportion of time that each dwelling is occupied by such a household. We achieve this using a Monte Carlo simulation of 50,000 independent dwelling histories.

## Data sources

Our model requires data on the proportion of households in each age bracket in which at least one member has a disability. We obtained these data via an ABS data consultancy request drawing on the 2022 Survey of Disability, Ageing and Carers (SDAC 2022). This provided national data for the number and proportion of households with at least one person with disability, and separately the number and proportion of households with at least one person with disability whose main long-term health condition is physical. The extract also included margin-of-error estimates, which we use to construct uncertainty bounds (low/base/high) around our rate inputs. We use these national data to estimate the corresponding rates for NSW and WA.

The model also projects how disability rates change over time within each age bracket. These trends are estimated from person-level "any disability" prevalence by age over 2003–2022, collated and published with SDAC 2022. Because these data are reported in finer age groups (e.g. 65–69 and 70–74) than our model brackets, we aggregate them into the model brackets by population-weighted averaging. Exact source cubes, tables, columns, and derivation rules for every input are listed in Appendix A (Table A1).

The final model inputs describe household mobility: the distribution of tenure lengths by age bracket, and the age distribution of households moving into a dwelling. Both derive from the ABS Housing Mobility and Conditions, Australia: Summary of Results, 2019–20 (the most recent release as at submission), which is drawn from the Survey of Income and Housing. The tenure-length distribution is taken directly from the published table; the in-mover age distribution is derived from the estimated number of households in each bracket with less than one year in their current dwelling, normalised across brackets. These data use ten-year age brackets from "15–24" up to "75 and over" (open-ended), which we adopt as the model's brackets throughout.

## Disability types

Our model considers two cohorts, one a subset of the other. The first is households with at least one person with any disability. Under the ABS SDAC definition, this is a person who has "any limitation, restriction or impairment which restricts everyday activities and has lasted, or is likely to last, for six months or more." This covers 39.6% of all households. We refer to this cohort as the "any disability" group; many of these households would benefit from the LHDS, though for others its features would be of little relevance.

The second cohort, a subset of the first, is households with at least one person who has a disability and whose main long-term health condition, the condition the ABS records as causing them the most difficulty, is physical. This covers 32.7% of all households and is the primary focus of our analysis. We refer to this group as the "physical disability" group.

We focus on the physical-disability group because it aligns most closely with the purpose of the LHDS. The standard is concerned with physical access, so the population it serves is best identified by the type of a person's condition rather than the severity of their disability. This use of a functional, physical measure was informed by Smith et al., who distinguished people with physical limitations from a narrower group that would have difficulties with self-care tasks.

## Disability rates over time

We simulate dwelling occupancy over a twenty year period and consider three treatments of how age-specific disability prevalence evolves over that period.

Throughout, the simulation base rates are household-level prevalence by age of the household reference person, drawn from the SDAC 2022. Where we project trends, the historical evidence is person-level "any disability" prevalence by age from the SDAC time series, aggregated from fine age groups into our seven model brackets by population-weighted averaging. Because no household-level time series with consistent age brackets was available, we assume household-level prevalence changes at the same proportional annual rate as the observed person-level series, as detailed below.

**No trend.** In our first specification, age-specific rates are held fixed at their 2022 levels for the whole horizon. The age-specific prevalences therefore do not change. However, the modelled lifetime probability that a dwelling ever hosts a household with disability still rises over the horizon, because occupants age into higher-prevalence brackets during their tenure (with conditions, once acquired, persisting) and because each dwelling is occupied by a succession of households. The in-mover age distribution is held constant, so no exogenous population ageing is imposed.

**Historical trends.** We then project rates forward using two historical windows: 2003–2022 and 2015–2022. The first is the longest period for which data are available. The second, although shorter, spans only surveys using a consistent disability definition, as the criteria changed before the 2015 SDAC. For each window we derive a linear trend for each age bracket from the two endpoint surveys, i.e. a constant annual increment equal to the difference between the 2022 and the start-year prevalence divided by the number of years in the window (19 and 7 years respectively). Increments may be positive or negative depending on the bracket. We express each bracket's increment as a proportional (relative) annual rate of change and apply that rate to the household-level 2022 base rate, additively each simulation year, bounding projected prevalence to [0, 1]. Because rates are updated each simulation year, under a rising trend a household may acquire a condition during its tenure even without ageing into a new bracket.

**Physical disability.** The historical time series covers "any disability" but not physical disability specifically. We therefore use the any-disability trend as a proxy for physical disability, assuming that physical-disability prevalence in each age bracket changes at the same proportional annual rate as any-disability prevalence in that bracket (rather than the same absolute percentage-point change). Concretely, the relative annual rate of change estimated from the person-level any-disability series is applied to the 2022 household-level physical-disability rate. Because physical disability is modelled as a subset of any disability, projected physical prevalence is additionally capped so it never exceeds projected any-disability prevalence in the same bracket and year.

## Population age distribution

Each dwelling is occupied by a succession of households, and every household is assigned an age when it moves in. We draw the household's age bracket from an in-mover age distribution, the age profile of households that have lived in their current dwelling for less than one year, rather than from the age profile of the standing population. Households that have recently moved are, by construction, younger on average than the population as a whole, and this distinction matters because younger households both differ in disability prevalence and tend to move again sooner. The in-mover distribution is derived from Housing Mobility and Conditions data as described above.

Having selected a bracket, we assign the household a single integer age drawn uniformly from within that bracket (for example, a household placed in 35–44 is equally likely to be any age from 35 to 44). This exact age determines how long the household remains in its current bracket before ageing into the next: a household entering at age 35 has ten years before reaching 45–54, whereas one entering at 44 has one year. The oldest bracket (75+) is open-ended and terminal; such households do not age into a further bracket.

The same in-mover distribution governs both the dwelling's initial occupant and every subsequent household that moves in over the 20-year horizon. This reflects the model's focus on newly built dwellings.

## Tenure estimation

The duration of a household's tenure is sampled from an age-specific tenure-length distribution, again taken from the same Housing Mobility and Conditions data. We use the household's age bracket at the point it moves in to select the relevant distribution, reflecting that younger households typically have shorter tenures. The distribution is specified over seven length-of-tenure buckets: less than 1, 1–2, 2–3, 3–4, 5–9, 10–19, and 20 or more years.

We first draw a bucket from the bracket-specific distribution, then convert it to a continuous duration by sampling uniformly within the bucket range (for example, a draw of "5–9 years" yields a duration uniform on [5, 9) years). The open-ended "20+" bucket is modelled as a uniform draw on [20, 30] years. Because the simulation horizon is itself 20 years, any tenure of 20 or more years means the household occupies the dwelling for the entire remaining horizon regardless of when it moved in; the precise duration drawn within this bucket therefore has no bearing on the results, and the choice of tail is immaterial.

When a tenure ends within the simulation horizon, the household moves out and a new household immediately moves in, with its age and tenure assigned afresh by the same procedure. This repeats until the 20-year horizon is reached. In sensitivity analysis we additionally lengthen the tenure of households that include a person with disability by a fixed factor (1.2×), to test whether reduced residential mobility among these households materially affects the results; in the main specification this factor is set to one.

## Uncertainty and sensitivity analysis

We characterise uncertainty in two distinct ways: sampling uncertainty in the base-year input rates, and structural uncertainty in our modelling assumptions.

**Input-rate uncertainty.** The ABS extract supplies a margin of error (a 95% confidence half-width) for each age bracket's disability rate. For every scenario we run three cases (low, base, and high) in which each base-year rate is set to its central estimate, its central estimate minus its margin of error, or its central estimate plus its margin of error, respectively (bounded to [0, 1]). This shift is applied to the year-2022 base rates for both the any-disability and physical-disability series; the proportional annual trend, which is estimated separately from the person-level historical series, is left unchanged (although the resulting absolute annual increments scale with the shifted base). For the low and high cases we move every age bracket's rate (and both disability categories) to the same edge of its margin of error simultaneously. This is deliberately conservative: because sampling errors in different age brackets are largely independent and would in practice partly cancel, our reported range is somewhat wider than a formally propagated 95% confidence interval would be.

**Structural sensitivity tests.** Alongside the central specification (in-mover seeding, no rate scaling, tenure unchanged), we run two structural variants:

- **Reduced residential mobility among households with disability.** Households containing a person with disability are assigned tenures 20% longer than the age-specific distribution implies (a tenure multiplier of 1.2). This tests whether lower mobility among these households, and hence longer occupancy of any home they reach, materially changes the lifetime probabilities. This reflects the possibility that such households are relatively less likely to move homes.

- **Self-sorting away from new (inaccessible) homes.** New dwellings are assumed to be inaccessible, and households with disability may avoid them. We represent this by scaling the base-year disability rates down by 10% (a rate multiplier of 0.9) for both categories, so that newly built dwellings begin with a lower disability prevalence than the general SDAC population.

The two structural variants are each evaluated under the no-trend specification, across all three uncertainty cases. The historical-trend treatments are applied to the central specification only. Structural and trend assumptions are therefore varied one at a time relative to the central case rather than fully crossed.

Our primary estimate is the central specification (in-mover seeding, no rate scaling, unchanged tenure) under the no-trend treatment with base-case input rates. The historical-trend windows, the low/high uncertainty cases, and the structural variants are reported as alternatives that bound this estimate.

## A worked example

To make the mechanics concrete, we trace a single simulated dwelling over the 20-year horizon under the no-trend specification. (All dice rolls below are illustrative draws; the reported results average over 50,000 independent dwellings.)

**Year 0: dwelling built, first household moves in.** The dwelling is newly built, so its first occupant is an in-mover. We draw an age bracket from the in-mover distribution; the modal group is 25–34 (probability ≈ 0.35), which we draw here. The household is assigned an exact age uniformly within the bracket, say 30, which leaves five years before it ages into 35–44. We then draw a tenure from the 25–34 tenure distribution. Suppose this returns the "5–9 years" bucket, giving a duration of 7.0 years. Finally, a weighted "dice roll" determines disability status using the published any-disability prevalence for 25–34 of 19.8%, and the draw returns no disability.

**Year 5: the household ages into the next bracket.** Still in the dwelling, the household crosses into 35–44. At this boundary it may acquire a disability it did not previously have, with probability (28.5% − 19.8%) / (1 − 19.8%) = 10.8%. Suppose the draw succeeds: the household now has a disability. A second draw then tests whether the main condition is physical, using the conditional probability for 35–44 of 18.9% / 28.5% = 66.3%. Suppose this returns not physical. The dwelling is now recorded as having ever hosted an any-disability household. Tenure length is not re-drawn when the household ages.

**Year 7: first household leaves, second moves in.** The 7-year tenure ends and a new in-mover is assigned. Suppose this household is drawn into 65–74 (age 70), where any-disability prevalence is 54.7%; the draw returns has disability, and the physical draw (conditional probability 51.3% / 54.7% = 93.8%) returns physical. The dwelling is now also recorded as having ever hosted a physical-disability household. The process continues — ageing within tenure, acquisitions at bracket boundaries, and new in-movers at each move-out — until year 20.

**Outcome.** For this single dwelling, the lifetime indicators are "ever hosted any disability = yes" (from year 5) and "ever hosted physical disability = yes" (from year 7). Averaging these binary outcomes over all 50,000 simulated dwellings gives our headline estimates: the probabilities that a new dwelling ever hosts an any-disability household and ever hosts a physical-disability household over 20 years.

In addition to these binary indicators, for each dwelling we record the share of the 20-year horizon during which it is occupied by an any-disability household, and likewise by a physical-disability household. Averaging across the 50,000 dwellings gives the mean proportion of time a dwelling is occupied by each group, alongside the lifetime probabilities.

**Under a trend specification.** The only difference is that the age-specific rates drift between move-outs. For example, under the 2003–2022 trend the any-disability rate for 65–74 declines slightly, as person-level prevalence fell over that window, so by year 7 the rate applied to the second household would be about 53.6% rather than 54.7%, with physical disability scaled down by the same proportional rate. The general population does not age in the background, and the in-mover age distribution is held fixed throughout; only the rates change with calendar time.
